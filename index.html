Are you a misclassified 1099 employee?

A misclassified employee is an a W2 employee masquerading as a 1099 employee. If you are misclassified, you can file a tax form to attempt to reconcile this with the IRS and shift the responsibility back where it belongs: on the employer.

Check below to see if you might be misclassified, and what you can do about it.

*this site is not legal advice, if you have any concerns you should ask a CPA or employment lawyer

What is Misclassification?

Basically, if you're hired as a 1099 employee but do work like a W2 employee - you're probably misclassified. Below are some examples of what the IRS would consider to be "W2 behavior." In short, if you're a 1099 Independent Contractor - you must have a certain degree of independence.
* no one thing in this list means you're an employee, but if you see a trend, be sure to talk to a CPA or lawyer
Job Training

Are you being brought in line with other employees via job training? This could imply an expectation to follow company guidelines like an employee. This could be training about company-specific procedures or anything unique to this company.

Type of Instruction

Are you being told where and when to work, what tools to use, or where to get supplies or services? Do you have "core business hours" or have to be in their office? Are you being asked to be "on call"? Do you do the same work as your peers?

Degree of Instruction

How detailed are your instructions? Are you being told specifically what to do at every turn? Do you have any say in what you do or how you do it?

Evaluation

Do you have work evaluations that evaluate how the work is being done? Does the company have a lot of say in how your work is done, rather than just caring about the end result?

Equipment

Does the company supply you equipment to work on? Are you given a laptop that you have to return at the end of your employment?

Benefits / Expenses

Do you receive benefits through the company (PTO, insurance, pension, sick pay, etc)? Does the company reimburse you for travel or other expenses?

Financial Gain

Do you collect a paycheck at a specific interval? Do you have no chance to make a profit or loss based on the quality of your work?

Hour Commitment

Are you required to work 40 hours a week? Do you have any potential to take on more jobs, or are you "exclusive" to this company?

Permanency

Are you expected to work at this company indefinitely, or is there a specific end date written into your contract?

Facts About Misclassification

* check the FLSA fact sheet - I copied important bits here, but there's more there

Getting a 1099 does not make you an independent contractor.

Signing a contract that says you're a 1099 does not make you an independent contractor.

There is no legal basis for an employer to misclassify an employee. They cannot legally do it.

You are not an independent contractor just because you have flexible hours or work from home.

No matter how you are paid - cash, check, on record or not - you may still be an employee.

"Common industry practice" is not a reason to misclassify you.

What does it mean for you?

* I live in one state in the US, so I might be mistaken on some things here
You Pay More Taxes

Normally, the employer will cover half of your tax burden. You will be responsible for this - federally, the difference is 7.5%. So you'll be paying 7.5% more on tax day than you would otherwise.

You Can't Get Unemployment/Comp

Independent contractors are not eligible for unemployment or workers compensation, nor will the employer have paid for either insurance for you.

You Can't Get Coverage

You also will not be able to get healthcare coverage as an employee, so you will have to pay for that yourself.

You Lose Workplace Rights

You don't have an entitlement to minimum wage, overtime pay, sick pay, breaks, and in some places, workplace amenities.

What can you do?

* I live in one state in the US, hopefully this is true for you, too
Read Your Contracts

If you have the capability, you can turn down contracts which intentionally misclassify you. If not, don't worry! You can resolve this at tax time.

Talk to Your Employer

They aren't all doing it on purpose. Tax code is arbitrarily difficult, and they might not understand what they're doing. Be careful, though, some companies may not respond well to this.

File Form SS-8

Submit an SS-8 with your taxes. It's tedious and can take up to 6 months for the IRS to make a decision, but it might be your only option.
